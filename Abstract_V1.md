Title:
A Multi-Agent AI Framework for Comprehensive Flood Disaster Response: Integrating Fine-Tuned Vision–Language Models, Predictive Geospatial Analytics, and Community-Driven Situational Awareness

Keywords: Multi-agent AI; Flood disaster response; Vision-language models; Geospatial analysis; Evacuation routing; Flood prediction; Shelter mapping; Crowd-sourced reporting; Offline resilience; Emergency decision-support

Abstract:

Flood disasters demand rapid, multi-dimensional situational awareness spanning hazard detection, infrastructure impact analysis, evacuation logistics, resource allocation, and community coordination. Conventional Geographic Information System (GIS) workflows address these requirements in isolation and typically consume hours of expert labour, by which time lives may already be lost. First responders therefore require a unified decision-support platform capable of fusing satellite imagery, live meteorological data, road network topology, and crowd-sourced field intelligence into actionable, continuously updated operational guidance within minutes. This study presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of an autonomous multi-agent artificial intelligence platform that delivers comprehensive end-to-end flood emergency response through a single user interaction, accessible to non-GIS-expert responders. The system architecture comprises four specialised agents orchestrated through the CrewAI framework with a Llama 3.2 reasoning backbone, collectively performing fourteen integrated response functions. A Qwen2-VL-7B vision–language model, fine-tuned via Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) on the AMD Instinct MI300X GPU under ROCm, performs satellite image damage classification and extracts damage-zone GeoJSON polygons. A live-data agent retrieves hourly precipitation from the Open-Meteo API to generate severity-scaled flood polygons, which are spatially intersected with OpenStreetMap road networks using GeoPandas to identify blocked segments by name, highway classification, and affected length. A routing agent computes optimal hazard-avoidant evacuation paths via NetworkX shortest-path algorithms on the unblocked OSMnx graph. Beyond these core capabilities, the platform extends its operational scope through several additional modules. An early warning subsystem continuously monitors precipitation trends and triggers severity-escalation alerts when rainfall thresholds are exceeded, enabling proactive rather than reactive emergency management. A predictive flood analytics module extends the Open-Meteo integration with seven-day forecast horizons and applies time-series trend analysis to project future flood severity, furnishing responders with anticipatory intelligence. An Overpass API-driven geospatial query engine locates nearby emergency shelters (schools, hospitals, community centres) and critical supply points (pharmacies, fuel stations, supermarkets) within configurable radii, displaying them as categorised point-of-interest layers with distance-ranked accessibility metrics. Safe zone recommendations are computed as the spatial complement of the flood polygon, filtered by road-network reachability and ranked by proximity to shelters and unblocked arterial corridors. A crowd-sourced flood reporting module enables field observers to submit geotagged incident reports — including severity assessments, photographic evidence, and descriptive observations — which are aggregated, visualised as a dedicated map layer, and incorporated into the AI-generated incident report. A missing person reporting subsystem allows responders to register individuals with last-known coordinates, descriptions, and contact information, rendered as geospatially referenced markers for coordinated search-and-rescue operations. An emergency SOS mechanism captures the user's geolocation, current flood severity context, and emergency contact information, generating a structured distress payload with embedded map coordinates for rapid dissemination. Emergency contact management permits responders to maintain and share contact registries that are automatically included in SOS transmissions and incident reports. A graceful-degradation strategy preserves operation under partial service availability: an offline emergency mapping module caches the road graph to persistent storage and exports the current operational map as a self-contained HTML document, ensuring continued situational awareness when network connectivity is intermittent or unavailable. A FastAPI backend exposes all geospatial and inference services to an interactive Streamlit command interface featuring multi-layer Folium map visualisation with real-time layer toggling. The deployed pipeline produced complete flood situational reports for target regions in under one minute, with the fine-tuned vision model returning damage GeoJSON in under five seconds per image. The system reliably identified blocked road segments and generated topologically valid evacuation routes around dynamically computed flood polygons. The findings demonstrate that coupling fine-tuned vision–language models with multi-agent orchestration, predictive meteorological analytics, and participatory community reporting can transform flood response from a fragmented, hours-long GIS workflow into a unified, minutes-long autonomous operation, offering a scalable and resilient template for AI-driven disaster decision-support in resource-constrained emergency settings.
